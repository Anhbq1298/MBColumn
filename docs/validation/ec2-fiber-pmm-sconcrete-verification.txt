9. ENGINEERING NOTES ON DISCREPANCIES
--------------------------------------
  ROOT CAUSE DIAGNOSIS
  The primary systematic discrepancy is that MBColumn gives LARGER moments than
  S-CONCRETE through much of the compression range. The current MBColumn EC2 path
  uses an EC2 rectangular stress block (lambda=0.8, eta=1.0) integrated through
  an 80x80 fiber mesh, with displaced concrete stress subtracted at reinforcing bars.
  Because the solver is already using a rectangular block, the remaining offset is
  not explained by a simple parabola-rectangle versus rectangular-block selection.

  a) Near-max-compression tail (|SC_N| > 88% of Nr_max, i.e., > 10 660 kN):
     At high compression, both tools' moment capacities approach zero. However,
     MBColumn's extrapolated compression branch remains larger than S-CONCRETE's
     reported Nr(max) of 12 113 kN.
     Consequence: at N=11 000–12 000 kN, S-CONCRETE moment is already near zero
     while MBColumn still shows 200–400 kNm. This creates 100–500%+ percentage
     differences. These are SPURIOUS — caused by different maximum capacities,
     not by a solver error. They are excluded from the practical-range statistics.

  b) Mid-range systematic offset (practical design range):
     MBColumn moments exceed S-CONCRETE by roughly 5-20% in much of the
     3 000-10 000 kN compression range, and more near the high-compression
     end. Possible explanations are hidden S-CONCRETE assumptions such as
     alpha_cc, member/slenderness reduction, minimum eccentricity, effective
     creep/second-order settings, or a different treatment of bar-displaced
     concrete and full-compression strain states.

  c) Maximum axial capacity:
     S-CONCRETE gives 12 113 kN. Possible reasons for a lower commercial
     maximum than the MBColumn generated curve include:
     (i)  S-CONCRETE uses alpha_cc = 0.80 (UK NA) rather than 0.85 (EN default).
          0.80*35/1.5 = 18.667 MPa → Nr ≈ 12 230 kN (closer to 12 113).
     (ii) S-CONCRETE may apply slenderness/member reduction before reporting the
          N-M curve; the file name includes 'slenderness' but the text export
          does not expose all internal settings.
     (iii)S-CONCRETE may cap the full-compression strain state differently.

  d) Pure bending (N=0):
     Agreement is good: theta=0 off by ~0.5%, theta=90 off by ~5% at N=0.
     Larger biaxial offsets indicate that angle definition, hidden reduction
     settings, or S-CONCRETE's reported 'Normal Moment' convention should be
     confirmed before treating this as a material-law-only difference.

  e) Near-peak-moment region:
     100-depth sweep (c=5 to 2400 mm) gives ~24 mm resolution near the balance point.
     Peak moment can shift by 1–5 kNm due to sweep density. Increasing
     NeutralAxisSamples to 200 would halve this error.

  f) Non-uniaxial theta angles (16, 106, 153, 207, 252, 293, 351°):
     Linear angular interpolation between ±2.5° flanking slices. For the 5° step
     and smooth PMM surface, error is typically < 0.3%.

  g) Symmetry check:
     S-CONCRETE shows identical curves at theta=0° and theta=180°, and at 90°/270°.
     MBColumn reproduces this by construction (symmetric section and symmetric rebar).
     MB theta=0 and 180 curves are identical, as expected. ✓

  h) Bar coordinate assumption:
     The comparison uses the explicit 16H25 perimeter layout shown in Section 2.
     If the S-CONCRETE model spaces side bars differently, biaxial angles will
     move even when pure Mx/My checks remain close.

  i) Stress block vs fiber integration:
     MBColumn uses fiber integration to approximate the rotated rectangular
     compression block. Mesh density and boundary clipping can shift moments,
     especially near peak and high-compression states.

  j) Moment formula used for this comparison:
     MB_M = sqrt(Mnx² + Mny²) = resultant moment [kNm].
     This equals |Mnx| at theta=0/180 and |Mny| at theta=90/270 (uniaxial cases).
     For biaxial angles, the projection formula |Mnx·cosθ + Mny·sinθ| would give
     approx. 41–58% of the true resultant, which is incorrect; sqrt is used instead.
